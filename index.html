10 Most Useful HTML Tags

  • div – For grouping and layout containers
  • span – For inline text styling and grouping
  • a – To create hyperlinks
  • img – To display images
  • header – For page or section headers
  • footer – For page or section footers
  • nav – For navigation menus
  • section – To group related content
  • h1...h6 – For headings and subheadings
  • p – For paragraphs of text

Important Header Tags for SEO

  • title – Appears in the browser tab and search results; should include your primary keyword and be 50–60 characters long.
  • h1 – The main heading of the page; use only one per page and include your main keyword.
  • h2 – Section headings that break content into major topics; good for secondary keywords.
  • h3 – Subheadings under h2 sections to provide more detailed structure.
  • meta name="description" – Provides a short summary (155–160 characters) of the page for search engines.
  • header – A semantic container for your page’s main heading and navigation; helps SEO understand the page introduction.
  • section and article – Group related content and define distinct content areas for better indexing.
  • strong and em – Highlight important keywords or phrases; signals emphasis to search engines.
  • Image alt attributes – Describe images with relevant keywords for accessibility and SEO.
  • Structured Data (JSON-LD) – Adds machine-readable info (schema.org) to enable rich results like star ratings or breadcrumbs.

This section demonstrates different CSS position properties: static, relative, absolute, and fixed. Each box is styled to show how the position property affects its placement on the page.

  • Static: This is the default positioning for every element. It stays exactly where it would normally appear in the document flow, one after the other, without any special positioning.
  • Relative: Similar to static, but you can shift it using top, left, right, or bottom. The space it originally occupied is still reserved, so other elements don't move into its spot. Example: The "Relative" box is moved inside the parent container towards the right, This is because it holds the left value.
  • Absolute: The element is taken out of the normal flow and positioned relative to its nearest positioned ancestor (the parent that has position: relative, absolute, or fixed). It will move around inside the parent if you change top, left, etc. Example: The "Absolute" box is placed at a custom spot inside the container — it will not push other boxes down because it no longer takes up space in the flow.
  • Fixed: The element is positioned relative to the entire browser window (viewport). It stays pinned even if you scroll the page. Example: The "Fixed" box stays visible as you scroll perfect for floating buttons.
